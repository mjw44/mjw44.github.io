Problem & Approach

Design Requirements

ExxonMobil required a variable-height release structure to position hydrogen sensors at controlled elevations during leak detection testing. The structure had to reach 30 feet, survive wind loading and high-pressure discharge events, and remain cost-effective for industrial deployment.

I led the design from requirements to delivery. I co-authored the BOM, selecting structural profiles, and coordinating electrical team integration to ensure all mechanical and operational criteria were met.

Thrust Loading — First Principles

Before selecting profiles, I needed to determine whether escaping high-pressure hydrogen gas would impose meaningful lateral loads. Using conservation of momentum on a control volume around the nozzle exit, I computed the reaction thrust force and compared it against the section's bending stiffness. The thrust was negligible relative to wind and self-weight, so it was correctly excluded from the governing load case.

Structural Validation

Euler Buckling — Critical Load

The governing failure mode for slender vertical members under axial load is Euler buckling. I applied the critical load formula to each beam segment at maximum extension, using the appropriate effective length factor for the end conditions at each joint.

The analysis confirmed that the critical buckling load exceeded the maximum expected axial demand. This accounted for self-weight and wind-induced lateral reactions. However, back-to-back unistrut was evaluated and implemented due to it's incredible rigidity.

Retrospective: What I Would Optimize

The prototype met all requirements and was delivered to ExxonMobil on schedule. A second iteration would target two specific levers:

01. Section Geometry

Shorter moment arms reduce base bending loads. Re-evaluating member layout could improve the stiffness-to-weight ratio and allow lighter profiles without compromising FoS.

02. Material Volume

The initial BOM was conservatively specified. Iterating various cross-section sizing against the validated load cases would reduce material cost while maintaining structural margins.
