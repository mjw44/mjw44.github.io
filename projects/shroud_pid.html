Closed-Loop Thermal Regulation System

PID-Controlled 18W Heat Load with Convective Optimization

Engineering Objective

Design and experimentally validate a closed-loop thermal regulation system capable of controlling an 18 W resistive heat load using forced convection and PID feedback control.

System Architecture

  • Arduino-based PID controller
  • 18 W resistive heat source
  • Custom ducted fan shroud
  • Thermistor-based temperature sensing
  • Custom PCB (KiCad)
  • MATLAB data processing suite

Control System Implementation

A PID controller was implemented to regulate temperature:

$$ u(t) = K_p e(t) + K_i \int e(t)\,dt + K_d \frac{de(t)}{dt} $$

Key challenges:

  • Thermal lag in system response
  • Fan stiction at low PWM values

Mitigation strategies:

  • Implemented minimum PWM floor (85/255) to overcome stiction
  • Tuned derivative gain to compensate for thermal inertia

Experimental Validation

Temperature data was collected and processed using MATLAB. The derivative of temperature with respect to time was computed using a MATLAB script.

$$ \frac{dT}{dt} \approx \frac{T_{i+1} - T_i}{\Delta t} $$

Analysis of the MATLAB data confirmed that the fan provided negligible cooling work due to poor airflow integration. To correct this, the second iteration will focus on optimizing the fan-to-heatsink distance, ensuring direct airflow impingement on the fins, and optimizing the resistor mounting to prioritize conductive heat transfer to the aluminum sink rather than the plastic housing.

Running the resistor at maximum heat generation and conducting a 1D transient heat transfer analysis reveals that the fan doesn't provide sufficient forced convection to facilitate cooling.

The second iteration will reduce the power output to the power resistor so that the fan can mitigate heat generation.

Iteration Two

Design Iteration

The second iteration reduced fan-to-heat-sink distance and redirected airflow through fin channels rather than across the surface.

This improved airflow alignment and increased effective convective performance.

Convective Heat Transfer Analysis

The initial cooling performance was limited by low convective heat transfer. The governing heat transfer relationship:

$$ q = h A (T_s - T_\infty) $$

The heat transfer coefficient \( h \) depends on the Nusselt number:

$$ Nu = \frac{h L}{k} $$

For forced convection:

$$ Nu = f(Re, Pr) $$

To increase \( Nu \), the duct geometry was redesigned with a converging cross-sectional area to increase local air velocity and Reynolds number:

$$ Re = \frac{\rho V L}{\mu} $$

Increasing Reynolds number increased convective heat transfer capability.

Engineering Takeaways

  • Convective geometry dominated system performance more than control tuning.
  • Control optimization cannot compensate for poor fluid mechanics.
  • Iterative prototyping revealed flow inefficiencies not captured analytically.
  • Experimental validation was essential for model refinement.
