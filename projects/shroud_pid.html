Closed-Loop Thermal Regulation

PID-Controlled 18W Heat Load with Convective Optimization

Engineering Objective

The goal was to design and experimentally validate a closed-loop thermal regulation system capable of controlling an 18 W resistive heat load using forced convection and PID feedback control.

System Architecture

  • Arduino-based PID controller
  • 18 W resistive heat source
  • Custom ducted fan shroud
  • Thermistor-based temperature sensing
  • Custom PCB (KiCad) & MATLAB data suite

Control System Implementation

A PID controller was implemented to regulate temperature based on real-time feedback:

$$ u(t) = K_p e(t) + K_i \int e(t)\,dt + K_d \frac{de(t)}{dt} $$

Key Challenges: Thermal lag in system response and fan stiction at low PWM values. I mitigated these by implementing a minimum PWM floor (85/255) and tuning derivative gain to compensate for thermal inertia.

Experimental Validation

Data was processed using MATLAB, computing the derivative of temperature to analyze real-time cooling rates.

$$ \frac{dT}{dt} \approx \frac{T_{i+1} - T_i}{\Delta t} $$

Iterative Design Refinement

Design Iteration

The second iteration changes heatsink orientation, increases contact area, and optimizes shroud length based on MATLAB heat transfer models.

Convective Heat Transfer Analysis

I utilized the Nusselt number and Reynolds relationship to redesign the duct geometry with a converging cross-sectional area to increase local air velocity:

$$ Nu = \frac{h L}{k} \quad \text{and} \quad Re = \frac{\rho V L}{\mu} $$

Engineering Takeaways

  • Geometry Matters: Convective geometry dominated system performance more than control tuning.
  • Model Refinement: Iterative prototyping revealed flow inefficiencies not captured analytically.
  • Validation: Experimental data was the only true way to confirm heat transfer coefficients.
