The Mission

The mission concept developed the WALL-E rover, an autonomous system designed to investigate subsurface ice deposits and volcanic history within a 10-mile radius of Elysium Mons on Mars. The primary science goal was to understand how biological systems, specifically Arugula microgreens, respond to Martian stressors.

Thermal Control System (TCS)

Thermal Equilibrium & Extreme Environment Management

As a Thermal Engineer, I ensured all rover payloads operated within allowable temperature ranges despite Martian extremes (120 K to 293 K). I established thermal equilibrium using active and passive mechanisms, including heaters and phase change materials.

Design Implementation:

  • Insulation: Used to protect components in sub-zero environments.
  • Phase Change Materials: Absorbed heat during high-load operations.
  • Mechanical Louvers: Automated regulation of heat radiation.

Collaborative Integration

Engineering a Mars mission required deep integration with multiple technical teams to resolve design conflicts:

  • Mechanical/Chassis: Coordinated the lining and protection of mechanical interfaces with predictive thermal equipment.
  • Electrical: Balanced the peak power draw (545 W) with the thermal load.
  • Science: Ensured the numerous instruments remained thermally isolated to prevent noise and interference.

Retrospective: Engineering Growth

Looking Back: I would further integrate automated temperature monitoring with the CDH unit to allow for autonomous "slow or pause" load-shedding when approaching thermal limits.
