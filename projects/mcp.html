Low-Carbon Backup Microgrid: UT Medical Complex

Sizing a 40 MW backup architecture bounded by a strict 30,000 sq ft footprint, optimizing hydrogen blending to maximize decarbonization.

Boundary Conditions

The Design Envelope

Architect a highly reliable 40 MW backup microgrid constrained to exactly 30,000 sq ft. The objective was to strictly maximize GHG abatement relative to a standard natural gas baseline while maintaining physical and economic feasibility.

Thermodynamic Architecture

System Flow & Integration

Daytime Synthesis PV Array → PEM Electrolyzer → H2 Storage → SGT-600 Turbine
Nighttime Synthesis Off-Peak ERCOT → PEM Electrolyzer → H2 Storage

The prime mover is a Siemens SGT-600 fed by a dynamically blended fuel stream.

40 MW
30,000 sq ft
10% H2
Grid-Assisted Hybrid

Engineering Approach

First-Principles Sizing

Engineered a parametric thermodynamic model mapping turbine heat rates across variable hydrogen blend ratios. Back-calculated hourly hydrogen mass flow to explicitly size upstream PEM electrolyzer draw, continuous water consumption, and required volumetric storage.

The Decarbonization Lever

Hydrogen blending decoupled carbon intensity from turbine output. The core analysis evaluated the maximum feasible volumetric blend ratio before storage CAPEX and land constraints forced structural failure.

Analytical Framework

Energy Balance Constraint

The model linked solar irradiance, electrolytic efficiency, and turbine demand into a continuous transient energy cascade.

System Demand Output

Mapped precise hourly H2 mass requirements against continuous multi-day loading.

Economic Optimization

MAC Curve Generation

Aggregated annualized CAPEX (PV, PEM, Steel Tanks) using a Capital Recovery Factor (CRF). Plotted against tonnes of CO2 avoided per year.

Optimization Target

Isolated the inflection point where cost per tonne of carbon abated is minimized before encountering diminishing capital returns.

Economic Tradeoffs

Single-Axis Decision Matrix

The Marginal Abatement Cost (MAC) curve quantified emissions reduction and infrastructure CAPEX on a single financial axis. This identified the highest-value hydrogen blend ratio bound by real-world physical limits, preventing massive overcapitalization on storage.

Analytical Bottlenecks

Analytical modeling exposed strict physical limits, forcing the architecture away from an islanded topology.

01 · PV Energy Density

Solar energy density over 30,000 sq ft is fundamentally insufficient to autonomously sustain >10% H2 blending for a 40 MW load.

02 · High-Pressure CAPEX

Volumetric requirements for long-duration, high-pressure hydrogen autonomy scaled costs prohibitively, eliminating 100% off-grid viability.

Architectures Eliminated

Long-duration Li-Ion failed cost/MWh thresholds. Micro-scale SMR with Carbon Capture lacked economy of scale. Wind resource profiles in Austin failed availability limits.

Long-Duration Li-Ion SMR / Blue H2 Wind Energy Islanded Autonomy

Final System State

A grid-assisted hybrid architecture operating at a mathematically optimized 10% hydrogen blend.

01. Topology Execution

Solar drives daytime electrolysis; off-peak ERCOT sustains baseline production. High-pressure storage acts as a transient buffer rather than bridging multi-day outages.

02. The 10% Optimum

Maximizes CO2 abatement within the fixed 30,000 sq ft footprint while remaining squarely on the linear, cost-effective region of the MAC curve.
