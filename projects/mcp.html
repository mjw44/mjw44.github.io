Low-Carbon Backup Microgrid for the UT Medical Complex

Engineering design study for a low-carbon backup power microgrid sized around a 40 MW reliability requirement, a fixed 30,000 sq ft land constraint, and hydrogen blending as the primary decarbonization lever.

Project Scope & Boundary Conditions

What the system had to do

The project required a highly reliable backup microgrid for the UT Medical Complex near the Fiddle Research Campus. The design had to guarantee 40 MW of backup generation capacity, reduce greenhouse gas emissions relative to a conventional natural gas baseline, and fit within exactly 30,000 sq ft of available land for renewable infrastructure.

Those constraints were treated as fixed, not negotiable. Every major design choice had to balance physical feasibility, emissions benefit, and capital realism.

Architecture & Approach

Ground-Up Modeling

Rather than relying on vendor estimates or rule-of-thumb sizing, the design was built around a ground-up thermodynamic model that traced the energy cascade from solar irradiance to turbine exhaust. That framework made it possible to quantify intermediate losses and defend the final architecture with first-principles calculations.

The model evaluated the turbine heat rate across multiple hydrogen blend ratios, then back-calculated hourly hydrogen demand. From that requirement, the analysis sized electrolyzer power draw, water consumption, and hydrogen tank volume needed to sustain continuous blended operation.

Why Hydrogen Blend Was Central

Hydrogen blending served as the primary pathway for reducing carbon intensity without abandoning the reliability and scale of a gas turbine backup system. The design question was not simply whether hydrogen could be added, but how far the blend could be pushed before land, storage, and capital constraints made the system impractical.

Thermodynamic Modeling Logic

Core Analytical Chain

The model linked solar generation, electrolyzer conversion, hydrogen storage, and turbine demand into a single energy balance. This allowed every hydrogen blend scenario to be evaluated on both a technical and economic basis.

Prime Analytical Output

Hourly hydrogen mass requirement for each blend ratio, with downstream sizing of electrolyzer capacity, water demand, and storage volume required to maintain that operating point.

System Logic

Daylight solar production supports electrolysis directly, while off-peak grid electricity sustains hydrogen production overnight when solar output is unavailable.

Economic Decision Framework

Marginal Abatement Cost Curve

The financial backbone of the study was a Marginal Abatement Cost (MAC) curve. Total system CAPEX was aggregated across solar panels, PEM electrolyzers, and high-pressure steel storage tanks, then annualized over a 20-year project life using a Capital Recovery Factor.

Key Metric

The MAC curve compared annualized system cost against tonnes of CO₂ avoided at each hydrogen blend ratio. The optimization target was the lowest achievable cost per tonne of carbon removed before capital costs entered a regime of steeply diminishing returns.

Economics & Tradeoffs

One Decision Axis for Cost and Carbon

The MAC framework made it possible to compare emissions reduction and capital expenditure on a single economic axis rather than treating them as unrelated objectives. That made the tradeoffs at each hydrogen increment legible both technically and financially.

This mattered because the project was not simply about maximizing the hydrogen fraction. It was about identifying the highest-value blend under real physical and cost limits, then justifying that point with transparent calculations.

Engineering Bottlenecks & Design Pivots

The most valuable output of the modeling phase was not a single optimum number, but the identification of the physical limits that forced the design away from initially promising ideas. Three constraints ultimately restructured the architecture.

01 · Footprint

Natural gas is extraordinarily energy-dense; solar power is not. The analysis showed that 30,000 sq ft of PV panels cannot generate enough electricity to sustain even a 10% hydrogen blend for a 40 MW turbine in isolation.

02 · CAPEX

High-pressure hydrogen storage is expensive at scale. Sizing tanks for long-duration autonomy drove costs into tens of millions of dollars, making extended off-grid storage economically indefensible for this use case.

Technologies Eliminated

Multi-day lithium-ion batteries were ruled out because long-duration storage cost was too high. SMR / blue hydrogen was excluded because micro-scale carbon capture lacks the economies of scale needed for viability, and wind was removed from consideration because Austin's wind resource profile is weak.

Final System Configuration

The 30,000 sq ft land constraint makes a purely off-grid solution physically impossible at the required power level. The final architecture is therefore a grid-assisted hybrid microgrid operating at a 10% hydrogen blend.

Project Focus

Predictive renewable energy analysis

This work centers on forecasting solar generation performance for a real site and translating that forecast into a clearer picture of long-term energy contribution.

The analysis considers how environmental conditions and system assumptions influence the resulting energy yield, making the document useful for both technical review and planning discussion.

01. Design Outcome

The solar array powers electrolysis during daylight hours, while off-peak ERCOT electricity runs the electrolyzers through the night to maintain hydrogen production. Storage is sized for short-term buffering rather than multi-day autonomy.

02. Why 10% Blend

The 10% hydrogen blend is the maximum achievable under the land constraint and the point on the MAC curve where cost per tonne of CO₂ abated is minimized. It preserves emissions benefit without letting storage cost dominate the architecture.
