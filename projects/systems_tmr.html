Thermal Regulation

The Problem

GPUs and power electronics sealed inside an aluminum chassis generate heat with no natural convection path. The core question: does the system reach thermal equilibrium before components throttle or fail?

Energy Balance

A network of NTC thermistors monitors internal air and component temperatures in real time. The governing lumped-capacitance equation (valid when Bi < 0.1) balances heat generation against convective loss through the chassis walls:

$$ \rho V C_p \frac{dT}{dt} = \dot{Q}_{gen} - h A (T_{int} - T_{ext}) $$

Lumped-capacitance model, valid when Bi < 0.1

Current Work

Live thermistor data is being correlated against the energy balance to extract the effective convective resistance of the chassis walls. If h·A is sufficient relative to Q̇gen, the system is passively safe. If not, active regulation is required.

Thermal Resistance Circuit

Simulation Pipeline: MATLAB to ANSYS to Physical Test

Stage 01: MATLAB

An iterative script sweeps h across flow regimes (laminar, transitional, turbulent) using the correct Nusselt correlation for each: Churchill-Chu for natural convection, Dittus-Boelter for forced. The lumped ODE is integrated at each h to find Tsteady, producing the minimum h needed to stay below the critical temperature limit.

Stage 02: ANSYS

With the critical h range established, a full transient thermal FEA resolves spatial gradients, hot spots, and conduction paths through fasteners that the lumped model cannot capture. Results confirm the MATLAB threshold or flag conditions requiring design changes.

Stage 03: Physical Test

Thermistor time-temperature traces from field operation are compared against both the MATLAB curve and ANSYS output. Agreement validates the model; discrepancies drive refinement of the thermal resistance assumptions and feed back to Stage 01.

Hull Optimization: Flow Visualization

Why Flow Visualization?

Drag is governed by wave-making resistance and wetted area, both of which depend on displacement depth and waterline geometry. Physical flow visualization lets us observe these effects directly across hull iterations rather than relying on simulation alone.

Testing Method

Two measurements are taken per hull iteration. A ruler records water level displacement, quantifying draft under load and confirming the buoyancy calculation. The waterline trace on the hull surface shows where the air-water interface sits, exposing regions of excess wetted area and geometric asymmetries that increase drag.

Each iteration is tested under the same payload and center-of-gravity condition. A lower, more uniform waterline across iterations means reduced wetted area and less drag. This loop runs in parallel with the hull CAD iterations below.

Next Steps

Once the thermal transient analysis is complete, thermal expansion cycles from testing will feed into a fracture mechanics study of the acrylic-to-aluminum bolt interfaces. Repeated heating and cooling differentially stresses the two materials, generating cyclic load at the fastener holes not captured by the static Goodman analysis. LEFM will estimate crack initiation life at those concentrations and set a maintenance inspection interval.
