The Thermal Challenge

In a sealed, watertight aluminum chassis, internal heat generation from high-draw processors (Jetson/GPU) and power electronics creates a "heat soak" effect. Unlike open-air systems, maritime robots must balance IP-rated sealing with thermal regulation.

The objective of this analysis is to determine the Time to Critical Temperature—quantifying how long the system can operate at peak load before internal air temperatures exceed the thermal throttling limits of the hardware.

Heat Transfer Fundamentals

The system is modeled as a transient heat power balance. I am analyzing the three-stage heat path:

  • Internal Convection: Energy transfer from chips to the internal air volume ($q = h A \Delta T$).
  • Chassis Conduction: Thermal resistance of the mounting interfaces and the aluminum walls.
  • External Convection: The chassis acting as a heat exchanger with the surrounding water—a massive heat sink with high convective efficiency.
Thermal Resistance Circuit
Q = \frac{T_{internal} - T_{water}}{\Sigma R_{thermal}}

Transient Analysis Workflow

To predict the temperature rise over time, I am utilizing a lumped capacitance model (where applicable) or a 1D transient numerical method to solve:

$$ \rho V C_p \frac{dT}{dt} = \dot{Q}_{gen} - \dot{Q}_{out} $$

This allows us to identify if the system reaches a steady-state temperature below the critical 80°C threshold, or if the mission duration is limited by a thermal "runway."

Validation Goals

Using onboard thermistors, the theoretical model will be validated against real-world mission data. This ensures that:

  1. The Aluminum chassis provides enough surface area for passive dissipation.
  2. The Internal air circulation (via fans) is sufficient to move heat to the chassis walls.
  3. The system can support the 120+ minute mission profiles required for autonomous observation.
