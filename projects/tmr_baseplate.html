The Design Challenge

The objective was to develop a baseplate for the TMR Autonomous Surface Vessel to house sensitive observation electronics. The design was governed by a strict set of industrial constraints:

  • Cost & Speed: Minimal budget with a need for immediate deployment.
  • TIW Accessibility: Manufacturing limited to tools available at the Texas Inventionworks (Maker Space).
  • Simple Geometry: Designed for rapid machining and easy field assembly.
  • Performance: Absolute prevention of deflection to maintain sensor calibration under multi-modal loading.

Material Selection & Logic

The choice of material was restricted to stock available for rapid turnaround: Wood, PLA/PETG, or Acrylic.

Acrylic (PMMA) was selected over 3D-printed polymers or wood due to its superior tensile strength and more predictable cyclic life in maritime conditions. While brittle, its transparency offered the added benefit of visual status-checks for internal hardware without disassembly.

Analytical Depth: Load Modeling

To ensure the plate could withstand more than just static weight, I modeled a comprehensive load profile:

1. Static & Dynamic Loads

Calculated forces from sensor weight, wave impact (crashing), and the maximum centripetal forces generated during high-speed turns.

2. Thickness Optimization

Using a custom spreadsheet, I iterated through thicknesses to find the minimum requirement that satisfied both deflection limits and the Factor of Safety.

The Goodman Shift

Breadth of Failure Analysis

The Goodman Criterion was applied to analyze the interaction between the constant bolt clamping force (Mean Stress) and the cyclic wave/turning loads (Alternating Stress).

The Technical Pivot: My analysis revealed that the initial torque specification (8 N-m) pushed the mean stress dangerously high. By reducing the torque, I shifted the operational point on the Goodman graph, effectively trading a small amount of clamping force for a significant increase in fatigue life.

Future Work: Fracture Mechanics

While the Goodman method ensures the part stays within the safe fatigue envelope, a more granular approach would involve Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics (LEFM).

Future iterations would analyze crack initiation and propagation rates $(da/dN)$ at specific stress concentrations ($K_t$). By modeling the number of cycles required for a microscopic surface craze to reach critical crack length, we could develop a predictive maintenance schedule for the ASV.
